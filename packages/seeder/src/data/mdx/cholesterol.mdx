<C>**DISCLAIMER:** THIS IS NOT MEDICAL ADVICE!</C>

<C>
  In the mid-20th century, cardiovascular disease surged to become the leading
  cause of mortality in America.
</C>

<C>
  The medical establishment, desperate for an explanation, needed a scapegoat,
  something tangible they could attribute to the epidemic, something simple
  enough to package into public health campaigns.
</C>

<C>And so, they fixated on cholesterol and saturated fat.</C>

<C>
  At the heart of this movement was{" "}
  <L href="https://www.sevencountriesstudy.com/about-the-study/investigators/ancel-keys/">
    Ancel Keys
  </L>
  , a physiologist whose work would shape decades of dietary policy and public perception.
  In the 50s, as heart disease became the leading cause of death in the U.S., health
  authorities sought a simple explanation and a clear enemy. Keys proposed that dietary
  fats, especially saturated fat, were the main driver of heart disease. His early
  1953 lecture plotted heart disease rates against fat intake in 6 countries, suggesting
  a striking correlation, but critics later noted he had data for 22 and only showed
  the subset that supported his theory. That criticism became the origin of the “cherry
  picking” accusation you see on the internet.
</C>

<C>
  Later, Keys launched the{" "}
  <L href="https://www.sevencountriesstudy.com/about-the-study/">
    Seven Countries Study
  </L>
  a prospective cohort study intentionally designed to compare populations with very
  different diets, rather than a retrospective selection from 22 nations. It did
  find relationships between saturated fat, serum cholesterol, and heart disease,
  but its ecological nature left major uncertainties. <L href="https://library.crossfit.com/free/pdf/1957_Yerushalmy_Hilleboe_Fat_Diet_Mortality_Heart_Disease.pdf">
    Yerushalmy & Hilleboe (1957)
  </L> warned that national averages don’t equal individual risk, a key example of
  ecological fallacy, and argued that the fat–heart hypothesis was far from settled.
  Both realities coexist: Keys’s early correlations were overinterpreted, and yet
  later mechanistic and clinical evidence did strengthen links between lipids and
  heart disease. What’s undeniable is that his framing of the issue, fat equals danger,
  set the stage for half a century of public-health orthodoxy and controversy alike.
</C>

<C>
  <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/cir.0000000000000510">
    **His work gained traction though.**
  </L>{" "}
  In{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/01.CIR.23.1.133">
    **1957–1961,**
  </L>
  the American Heart Association (AHA) released and then formalized statements suggesting
  that diet played a significant role in heart disease, placing heavy emphasis on
  reducing total fat intake. By 1961, the AHA doubled down, aggressively pushing
  Americans to **slash saturated fat, lower cholesterol** and <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/cir.0000000000000510">
    **swap fats for polyunsaturated vegetable oils**
  </L>, a disastrous move that paved the way for countless health issues. This was
  the first major institutional push toward
  <L href="https://www.heart.org/-/media/files/healthy-living/company-collaboration/inap/fats-white-paper-ucm_475005.pdf">
    **low-fat, high-carb dietary policies**
  </L>
  , speaking of vegetable/seed oils, this topic is so controversial that I might
  need to drop a whole post about it.
</C>

<C>
  But anyway, the{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9794145/">**1970s,**</L>{" "}
  push to demonize cholesterol reached its apex. In
  <L href="https://catalog.hathitrust.org/Record/000321104">**1977,**</L> the U.S.
  Senate Select Committee on Nutrition and Human Needs, led by <L href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_McGovern">
    **Senator George McGovern,**
  </L> published the <L href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_Goals_for_the_United_States">
    **Dietary Goals for the United States.**
  </L> This document formalized what had previously been just recommendations, now,{" "}
  it was an <L href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_Goals_for_the_United_States">
    official{" "}
  </L> policy.
</C>

<C>
  The guidelines urged Americans to reduce total fat intake from 40% to 30% of
  total calories, limit saturated fat to no more than 10%, restrict cholesterol
  intake, and increase carbohydrate consumption.
</C>

<C>
  And then, of course, came the studies that{" "}
  <L href="https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/38/32/2459/3745109">
    **"cemented" LDL cholesterol as the enemy.**
  </L>{" "}
  The{" "}
  <L href="https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/38/32/2459/3745109">
    **Brian Ference analysis**
  </L>{" "}
  aggregated data from major cholesterol studies and concluded that{" "}
  <L href="https://academic.oup.com/eurheartj/article/38/32/2459/3745109">
    **higher LDL levels correlated with an increased risk of atherosclerosis.**
  </L>{" "}
  The{" "}
  <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2017.03.033">
    **PESA trial**
  </L>{" "}
  used advanced imaging technology to show that{" "}
  <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2017.03.033">
    **even LDL levels as low as 110 mg/dL**
  </L>{" "}
  were associated with arterial plaque formation. The{" "}
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/216169">
    **CARDIA study**
  </L>{" "}
  tracked{" "}
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/216169">
    young men
  </L>{" "}
  from age **18** and found that those with the highest LDL levels developed
  cardiovascular issues much earlier. The{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa0807646">
    **JUPITER trial**
  </L>{" "}
  showed that lowering LDL through statins{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa0807646">
    **reduced the risk of cardiovascular events,**
  </L>{" "}
  and the **Vietnam War veterans' autopsy{" "}
  <L href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3346951/">study</L>** found that
  young soldiers who died in combat had significantly more arterial plaque{" "}
  <L href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/3346951/">
    **if they had higher cholesterol levels.**
  </L>
</C>

<C>
  But decades later, investigative reporting revealed internal documents showing
  how **Big Sugar strategically{" "}
  <L href="http://nytimes.com/2016/09/13/well/eat/how-the-sugar-industry-shifted-blame-to-fat.html">
    funded reasearch
  </L>{" "}
  to shift the blame for heart disease away from sugar and onto fat** . They
  knew that if fat was labeled as the problem, sugar and refined carbohydrates
  would{" "}
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2548255">
    **fill the gap in the food supply.**
  </L>{" "}
</C>

<C>
  When McGovern's committee released its dietary guidelines, they made an
  audacious claim:{" "}
  <L href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_Goals_for_the_United_States">
    **"Americans have nothing to lose by adopting a low-fat diet."**
  </L>
  , although there were <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/01.CIR.23.1.133">
    **no conclusive evidence**
  </L> proving that reducing dietary fat would **prevent** heart disease, the committee
  pushed the idea as if it were **settled science.** regardless of the evidence.
</C>

<C>
  The{" "}
  <L href="https://health.gov/our-work/nutrition-physical-activity/dietary-guidelines">
    **USDA immediately followed suit,**
  </L>{" "}
  formalizing these recommendations and reinforcing the idea that fats and oils
  should be eaten **“sparingly”** . The public was told that fat was the root
  cause of heart disease, obesity, and metabolic disorders.
</C>

<C>
  By the 1980s, the{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9794145/">
    **low-fat dogma had become deeply embedded in American culture.**
  </L>{" "}
  Grocery store shelves were flooded with **low-fat, high-carb processed
  foods,** all marketed as “heart-healthy.” And the world followed suit.
</C>

<C>
  The food industry **benefited immensely** from this shift. Fat, precisely
  animal fat, is expensive to produce and difficult to store long-term.
  Carbohydrates, on the other hand, are **cheap, highly profitable, and easy to
  process, and very addictive.**
</C>

<C>
  By demonizing saturated fat and promoting grain-based foods, the agricultural
  and processed food industries found themselves in an{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9794145/">
    **unparalleled position of economic advantage.**
  </L>
</C>

<C>
  The pharma industry also stood to gain, as this diet shift lead to a surge in
  chronic illness, thus the need for medication.
</C>

<C>So we can safely say, they were printing money.</C>

<C>But cracks in the narrative were forming.</C>

<C>
  For decades, cholesterol has been scapegoated as the primary driver of heart
  disease, but that narrative collapses under serious scrutiny. The problem
  isn't the existence of data linking cholesterol to heart disease, it's how
  that data has been interpreted and weaponized into dogma. The mainstream
  cholesterol hypothesis reduces an immensely complex metabolic condition into a
  single biomarker, ignoring the far more powerful influences of overall
  metabolic health. LDL, the particle that your doctor call “bad” cholesterol on
  its own is not the cause of heart disease, its role depends on context (more
  below).
</C>

<C>
  Decades of research have shown this clearly. As early as the 1950s, landmark
  studies began to contradict the LDL-centric model. The{" "}
  <L href="https://FraminghamHeartStudy.org/">Framingham Heart Study</L>, the{" "}
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/648181">
    Honolulu Heart Program
  </L>
  , and later meta-analyses consistently revealed that total and LDL cholesterol
  alone fail to predict heart disease across diverse populations once other metabolic
  variables are accounted for. Many individuals with “high” LDL remain healthy when
  insulin sensitivity, triglyceride-to-HDL ratio, and inflammation are favorable,
  while those with “normal” LDL but poor metabolic health are at higher risk. Yet
  for decades, medicine doubled down on the simpler story for a reason or another.
  The truth, even back then, was already in the data, it just required looking beyond
  cholesterol.
</C>

<C>
  Some researchers started{" "}
  <L href="https://www.atkins.com/how-it-works/atkins-40">
    **challenging the low-fat paradigm.**
  </L>{" "}
  One of the most vocal figures was{" "}
  <L href="https://www.atkins.com/how-it-works/robert-atkins">
    **Dr. Robert Atkins.**
  </L>
  He argued that the <L href="https://www.atkins.com/how-it-works/science-behind-atkins">
    **real culprits of obesity and metabolic disease weren’t fats, but processed
    carbohydrates.**
  </L> Atkins promoted a <L href="https://www.atkins.com/how-it-works/atkins-20">
    **high-fat, low-carb diet,**
  </L> directly opposing the establishment’s guidelines. He pointed out that
  <L href="https://www.atkins.com/how-it-works/science-behind-atkins">
    **as fat intake declined, obesity and diabetes rates skyrocketed.**
  </L>{" "}
  The rise of refined carbohydrate consumption, fueled by low-fat dietary
  recommendations,{" "}
  <L href="https://www.atkins.com/how-it-works/science-behind-atkins">
    **correlated almost perfectly with the obesity epidemic of the 80s and the
    90s.**
  </L>
</C>

<C>
  By the early 1990s, studies were showing that{" "}
  <L href="https://ajcn.nutrition.org/article/S0002-9165(23)01670-2/fulltext">
    **low-fat diets weren’t solving anything,**
  </L>{" "}
  and one of the most crucial realizations from recent research is that
  cholesterol is not inherently harmful.
</C>

<C>
  We must understand what Cholesterol actually is, it's an essential molecule
  required for various bodily functions, including the production of hormones,
  vitamin D synthesis, and cell membrane integrity. The body itself produces
  cholesterol because it is so vital to survival. The conventional focus on
  total cholesterol levels as a primary indicator of heart disease risk fails to
  take into account the complexity of cholesterol metabolism and the
  **numerous** factors that influence cardiovascular health.
</C>

<C>
  The standard narrative often emphasizes that low-density lipoprotein (LDL), is
  the primary driver of heart disease. The notion that all LDL is inherently
  dangerous is misleading. LDL is not a single uniform entity, it exists as
  particles that vary in size and density. Small, dense LDL particles are more
  atherogenic (means something that promotes the formation of atherosclerosis,
  which is the buildup of fatty plaques inside your arteries) than large,
  buoyant LDL particles because they are more likely to penetrate the arterial
  wall and are more susceptible to oxidative modification. Prospective data from
  the Framingham Offspring cohort and later meta-analyses link higher small,
  dense LDL to greater coronary risk, while patterns with larger, more buoyant
  LDL are less adverse when particle number is low. Checkout
  <L href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20431054/">Clin Chem 2010</L> and <L href="https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0241993&type=printable">PLOS One 2020 meta-analysis</L>
</C>

<C>
  A pivotal prospective study in{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/01.cir.95.1.69">
    Circulation
  </L>{" "}
  in 1997 reported that men with predominantly small, dense LDL had about 3x
  higher risk of ischemic heart disease than those with larger LDL patterns,
  independent of several covariates. That is the canonical particle-size result
  people usually cite. Separately, a{" "}
  <L href="https://www.acpjournals.org/doi/10.7326/M13-1788">
    2014 Annals of Internal Medicine meta-analysis
  </L>{" "}
  found no clear association between dietary saturated fat and coronary risk, a
  nutrition finding often conflated with blood lipid particle biology.
</C>

<C>
  **Particle size** refers to the physical characteristics of LDL particles that
  carry cholesterol through the bloodstream. LDL comes in different forms: small
  and dense, or large and buoyant. The small, dense particles are more likely to
  slip between endothelial cells, get trapped in the arterial wall, and become
  oxidized, which triggers inflammation and plaque formation. However, the risk
  linked to small LDL is often a reflection of *particle number*, not just size.
  A person with many LDL particles will naturally have more small ones, so what
  truly matters is *how many atherogenic particles are circulating*, not their
  size alone.
</C>

<C>
  **LDL-C** represents the total amount of cholesterol contained within
  low-density lipoprotein particles circulating in the blood, while
  **Apolipoprotein B (apoB)** is the structural protein present on every
  atherogenic lipoprotein, including LDL, VLDL, and remnant particles. Because
  each particle carries exactly one apoB molecule, measuring apoB provides a
  direct count of how many cholesterol-carrying particles are actually
  interacting with your arterial walls. The higher the apoB, the greater the
  exposure of the endothelium to these particles and the higher the risk of
  atherosclerosis. When LDL-C and cardiovascular risk do not align, modern
  guidance relies on apoB or non-HDL-C, since both capture total atherogenic
  particle burden more accurately than LDL-C alone. Systematic reviews and
  expert consensus consistently identify apoB as the superior marker for
  discordance. See the{" "}
  <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.07.006">
    ACC 2022 Expert Consensus
  </L>
  , <L href="https://www.lipidjournal.com/article/S1933-2874(24)00240-X/fulltext">
    J Clin Lipidol 2024 review
  </L>, and <L href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40681368/">
    Systematic review 2025
  </L>.
</C>

<C>
  This distinction matters because LDL-C and apoB do not always rise or fall
  together. Someone may have “normal” LDL-C but still carry a high number of
  smaller, denser, more atherogenic particles. ApoB captures this hidden risk by
  measuring the total number of particles rather than just the cholesterol they
  carry. High apoB means a high traffic of lipoproteins capable of penetrating
  and damaging arterial walls, regardless of whether LDL-C looks ideal or the
  particles are labeled “large and fluffy.” Read{" "}
  <L href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31642874/">2019 review</L> and a{" "}
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamacardiology/article-abstract/2786334">
    2022 JAMA Cardiology analysis
  </L>
  .
</C>

<C>
  **High-density lipoprotein (HDL)** aka "good cholesterol" plays a protective
  role through a process called *reverse cholesterol transport*. It works like a
  cleanup crew, collecting excess cholesterol from arterial walls and moving it
  back to the liver for recycling or disposal. This helps prevent cholesterol
  accumulation in blood vessels. However, the amount of HDL cholesterol alone
  does not fully represent its protective capacity, because HDL function and
  overall lipid balance matter more than the number itself.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  **Triglycerides** are the main form of fat used for energy storage in the
  body. They are carried in the bloodstream inside lipoproteins such as VLDL,
  which the liver produces when carbohydrate intake or insulin resistance is
  high. When triglycerides rise, it often reflects a metabolic environment where
  the liver is overproducing VLDL particles. These particles later lose their
  triglyceride content and become smaller, denser LDL particles that are more
  likely to enter artery walls and cause plaque buildup.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  The **triglyceride-to-HDL ratio** is one of the strongest indicators of
  metabolic health because it reflects how efficiently the body processes fat
  and sugar. A low ratio signals good insulin sensitivity and balanced lipid
  metabolism, while a high ratio points to insulin resistance, excess VLDL
  production, and a rise in small, dense LDL particles that accelerate
  atherosclerosis. Even if LDL-C levels appear normal, a high
  triglyceride-to-HDL ratio often reveals underlying metabolic dysfunction and
  elevated cardiovascular risk. High triglycerides combined with low HDL almost
  always indicate an atherogenic profile, which is why many clinicians use this
  ratio as a quick metabolic check. See the 2022{" "}
  <L href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0939475321005469">
    systematic review
  </L>{" "}
  and{" "}
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9666180/">
    cohort synthesis
  </L>{" "}
  for supporting data.
</C>

<C>The triglycerides to HDL ratio is calculated as:</C>

<C>
  <strong>Ratio = Triglycerides / HDL</strong>
</C>

<C>
  Interpreting this ratio depends on population and units, but consistent adult
  cut points in mg/dL are roughly:
</C>

<C>
  • <strong>&lt; 2</strong>: generally favorable
  <br />• <strong>2 to 3</strong>: borderline, warrants context
  <br />• <strong>&gt; 3 to 3.5+</strong>: often flags higher risk or insulin
  resistance
</C>

<C>
  A classic analysis proposed a{" "}
  <L href="https://cardiab.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/1475-2840-7-4">
    3.5 cutoff
  </L>{" "}
  for the small, dense LDL phenotype in adults, while newer cohorts report
  sex-specific thresholds around{" "}
  <L href="https://bmcgeriatr.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12877-023-04040-8">
    3.5 in men and 3.0 in women
  </L>
  , with variation across ethnicity and age.
</C>

<C>
  Multiple studies show that a higher triglyceride to HDL ratio associates with
  cardiovascular events and with insulin resistance independent of LDL levels.{" "}
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2664115/">Clinics 2008</L>,{" "}
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8418107/">
    Frontiers in Cardiovascular Medicine 2021
  </L>{" "}
  and clinical reviews summarizing the metabolic signal in this ratio. Improving
  the ratio usually means addressing metabolic health at large, not just chasing
  LDL.
</C>

<C>
  Now let's talk about statins. Statins are among the most prescribed drugs for
  lowering LDL cholesterol.
</C>

<C>
  **Statins** reduce LDL levels by inhibiting the liver enzyme **HMG-CoA
  reductase**, which is a key step in the body’s own cholesterol production
  pathway. When this pathway slows down, the liver compensates by increasing the
  number of LDL receptors on its surface, pulling more LDL particles out of
  circulation.
</C>

<C>
  **PCSK9 inhibitors** (funny how it sounds like{" "}
  <L href="https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc2315">PKCS7</L> from
  cryptography) take cholesterol control to another level. PCSK9 is a protein
  that tags LDL receptors for destruction, cutting short their job of clearing
  cholesterol from the blood. When you block PCSK9 with monoclonal antibodies
  like evolocumab or alirocumab, those receptors stay alive and keep cycling
  back to the liver’s surface, grabbing more LDL each time. The result is a
  massive cleanup of circulating LDL and drops of 50–70% beyond what statins
  alone can achieve.
</C>

<C>
  When clinicians refer to **“very low LDL”**, they typically mean **LDL-C
  levels below about 25 mg/dL**, which are usually achieved through a
  combination of a statin and a PCSK9 inhibitor. At those levels, circulating
  apoB and LDL particle counts become extremely low, and the process of
  atherosclerosis can not only come to a stop but in some cases begin to
  regress.
</C>

<C>
  The net benefit of **statins** depends on a person’s baseline cardiovascular
  risk. Some analyses point out that headline *relative-risk* reductions can
  exaggerate the benefit for low-risk individuals, because the *absolute* number
  of events prevented is small.{" "}
  <L href="https://ebm.bmj.com/content/23/4/181">BMJ Evidence-Based Medicine</L>{" "}
  and a 2022 systematic review both emphasize this gap between relative and
  absolute effects.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  The{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa0807646">
    **JUPITER trial**
  </L>{" "}
  explored this nuance. It tested rosuvastatin in people who had normal LDL
  levels but elevated **hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein)**, which is
  a blood marker of systemic inflammation. hs-CRP reflects the activity of the
  immune system inside blood vessels and tissues where higher levels indicate
  chronic low-grade inflammation that contributes to atherosclerosis. In
  JUPITER, participants with higher hs-CRP benefited from statins, not because
  their LDL was high, but because their inflammation increased overall vascular
  risk.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  In primary prevention (no prior cardiovascular disease), the absolute benefit
  is proportional to baseline risk. The{" "}
  <L href="https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/statin-use-in-adults-preventive-medication">
    USPSTF 2022
  </L>{" "}
  concludes statins provide a moderate net benefit for adults 40–75 with risk
  factors and ≥10% 10-year risk, and a small net benefit for 7.5–10% risk. Their
  evidence review reports small absolute reductions in all-cause mortality, MI
  and stroke, with harms that are “at most small.” Large meta-analyses (CTT)
  also show consistent event reduction per mmol/L LDL lowering. For low-risk
  people, absolute gains are modest.
</C>

<C>
  In secondary prevention (established ASCVD), benefits are clear. Intensive LDL
  lowering reduces events across trials, and nonstatin add-ons reinforce the
  LDL-causality signal:{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1410489">IMPROVE-IT</L>{" "}
  (ezetimibe added to statin) and{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1615664">FOURIER</L>{" "}
  (PCSK9 inhibitor) both reduced major events when LDL was lowered further on
  top of statins.
</C>

<C>
  For older adults though, evidence is heterogeneous. An often-cited 2016 review
  found LDL-C inversely or not associated with mortality in many ≥60-year
  cohorts, but this observational signal is contested and does not negate
  randomized-trial benefits in higher-risk elders. The USPSTF judges evidence
  insufficient for starting statins after age 75 without CVD, while newer
  analyses suggest relative benefit persists ≥70 when baseline risk is high.
  Read about
  <L href="https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/6/6/e010401">BMJ Open 2016</L>, <L href="https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/statin-use-in-adults-preventive-medication">
    USPSTF 2022
  </L>, <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2023.07.027">
    JACC 2023
  </L>.
</C>

<C>
  LDL-C alone is an imperfect proxy. When markers disagree, particle number
  (apoB / non-HDL-C) tracks risk better than LDL-C or particle size. Modern
  guidance treats high apoB and high Lp(a) as risk-enhancing factors that can
  tilt decisions toward therapy. See the{" "}
  <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.07.006">
    2022 ACC Expert Consensus
  </L>{" "}
  and a{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIRCULATIONAHA.124.068885">

<C>
  Adverse effects exist but are generally uncommon at population level. The most

<C>
  Statins are high-value for secondary prevention and for primary prevention
  when baseline risk is meaningfully elevated, they add little for truly
  low-risk people with excellent metabolic markers. Mechanistically, benefit
  maps most closely to lowering the burden of atherogenic particles (apoB), with
  inflammation markers like hs-CRP refining who benefits. But first let me walk
  you through the science behind the obsession over excessively lowering
  cholesterol levels.

<C>
  Some people say that low LDL will basically fry your brain, I've looked into
  the topic and can safely say it won't. Best randomized evidence does not
  support that. In the EBBINGHAUS trial, a cognitive substudy of FOURIER, people
  on evolocumab who reached very low LDL did the same on formal thinking and
  memory tests as those on placebo for about 2.2 years.{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1701131">NEJM 2017</L>,{" "}
  <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2020.03.039">JACC 2020</L>.
</C>

<C>
  Systematic reviews and meta-analyses that pool trials and large cohorts find
  no cognitive harm signal from statins or from intensive LDL lowering that
  includes PCSK9 inhibitors. Some analyses even suggest statin users have equal
  or lower dementia risk overall, though observational effects need caution.{" "}
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11736423/">
    Systematic review 2025
  </L>
  , <L href="https://academic.oup.com/ageing/article/54/8/afaf219/8223250">
    Meta-analysis 2025
  </L>, <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9834631/">
    Meta-analysis of PCSK9 safety 2023

<C>
  The "against" side exists and should be stated though. Early extension trials
  and some pooled safety summaries reported a small excess of patient-reported
  neurocognitive events with PCSK9 inhibitors and raised the question. More
  recent and larger randomized cognitive testing did not confirm a causal
  problem, but this is why pharmacovigilance continues.{" "}
  <L href="https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40256-023-00604-6">

  </L>.

<C>
  Genetics gives a causal lens. Mendelian randomization studies use natural
  genetic differences to mimic the effects of lifelong drug exposure. This helps
  scientists understand whether certain biological pathways directly cause
  benefits or harm over time. Variants that reduce the activity of the **HMG-CoA
  reductase** gene simulate what statins do. This enzyme, called
  **3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA reductase**, controls the main step in the
  liver's cholesterol production process. When it is blocked, cholesterol
  synthesis slows down and the liver creates more LDL receptors to clear LDL
  particles from the blood.
</C>

<C>
  Variants that reduce **PCSK9** function work in a similar way to PCSK9
  inhibitors, allowing more LDL receptors to stay active on liver cells and
  lower LDL cholesterol. Long-term studies of people who naturally carry these
  variants from birth show that having lifelong lower LDL levels does not harm
  brain function or memory. A few studies found a very weak association between
  HMG-CoA–related variants and small changes in cognition, but the effect was
  minimal and clearly outweighed by the protection against heart disease.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Genetic data show that keeping LDL low for life through reduced HMG-CoA
  activity or PCSK9 function does not cause cognitive problems. The body
  tolerates low LDL levels well, and the cardiovascular benefits are far greater
  than any theoretical risks. See{" "}
  <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2022.05.041">JACC 2022</L>{" "}
  and its <L href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35953131/">PubMed record</L>.

<C>
  What about studies in older adults linking lower LDL to worse outcomes or more
  dementia? Those are observational and vulnerable to reverse causation, because
  illness can drive cholesterol down before diagnosis. A widely cited review in
  adults 60 and older found inverse or null associations between LDL-C and
  mortality, but that does not overturn randomized evidence of cardiovascular
  benefit, nor does it prove that low LDL causes cognitive harm.{" "}
  <L href="https://bmjopen.bmj.com/content/6/6/e010401">BMJ Open 2016</L>.
</C>

<C>
  Very low LDL achieved with modern therapy has not been shown to impair
  thinking or memory in randomized testing over several years. The EBBINGHAUS

  LDL fell below 20 mg/dL. Pooled reviews to date do not show a neurocognitive
  harm signal from statins or intensive LDL lowering.{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1701131">NEJM 2017</L>,{" "}
  <L href="https://www.jacc.org/doi/10.1016/j.jacc.2020.03.039">JACC 2020</L>,{" "}

<C>
  The conversation becomes far more personal when looking at cases like mine,
  because I fit the Low Mass Hyper-Responder (LMHR) profile perfectly. I'm lean,
  athletic, insulin-sensitive, and my LDL cholesterol is very high (almost
  double normal range) while my HDL is strong and triglycerides stay extremely
  low. This pattern is not what medicine traditionally associates with risk. In
  most populations, high LDL travels alongside high triglycerides, low HDL,
  excess fat mass, and inflammation. In my case, the rest of the markers point
  in the opposite direction, which makes interpretation tricky.
</C>

<C>
  The science surrounding **Low Mass Hyper-Responders** is still emerging. Most
  evidence so far comes from small observational cohorts and self-reported data,
  not long-term controlled studies. The **Lipid Energy Model** is the leading
  hypothesis explaining why LDL rises in people like me. When carbohydrate
  intake drops, the body turns to fat as its main energy source. The liver
  exports more triglyceride-rich VLDL particles to deliver energy to tissues.
  Those triglycerides are rapidly used by muscles and other cells, leaving
  behind cholesterol-rich LDL particles in the bloodstream. This results in
  higher LDL cholesterol even though overall metabolic health improves. The
  mechanism makes physiological sense but is still theoretical, and we do not
  yet know whether the LDL rise from this process carries the same vascular risk
  as LDL elevation driven by inflammation or insulin resistance.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Because the academic research is still in its early stages, most of what we
  know about LMHRs comes from real people gathering their own data in real time
  in the trenches. The **
  <L href="https://www.facebook.com/share/g/1D1GmCUkXi/">LMHR Facebook group</L>
  ** now includes more than thirteen thousand members who track their lab
  results, diet changes, and imaging outcomes. It functions as a citizen-science
  community, collecting large sets of data that traditional institutions have
  not yet studied in depth. Many of us, myself included, use these shared
  results to understand whether the pattern is benign adaptation or potential
  risk.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  What makes this field difficult is that most LMHRs are young, physically fit,
  and metabolically stable, which means cardiovascular events are rare and
  studies take decades to show clear outcomes. Some differences in genetics,
  duration of carbohydrate restriction, and lipid transport may also affect
  individual results. The limited research available suggests that the biology
  behind our elevated LDL may differ from the mechanisms seen in conventional
  hyperlipidemia, where inflammation, oxidative stress, and endothelial damage
  amplify risk.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Until more long-term data exist, it is most reasonable for LMHRs to treat the
  phenotype as an open question rather than a settled fact. That means
  maintaining excellent metabolic health, staying active, continuing dietary
  self-experimentation, and tracking objective imaging data instead of assuming
  either immunity or danger. The emerging LMHR community is helping fill the
  research gap, and as the science grows, we may finally learn whether this
  adaptation is a harmless reflection of fat metabolism or an early warning
  signal that should not be ignored. Report back in 10 years.
</C>

<C>
  Anyway, back to the main point, how to act on risk. Do not chase zero without
  a plan, but do address atherogenic particle burden when baseline risk is
  clearly elevated. The smart goal is to bring **apoB** to a level that aligns
  with your overall risk, then manage the other drivers of disease: glucose
  control, blood pressure, inflammation, sleep, physical conditioning, body
  composition, and smoking status. In people with established cardiovascular
  disease, intensive LDL lowering reduces heart attacks and strokes without any
  evidence of cognitive harm. In low-risk individuals, the absolute benefit is
  smaller, so decisions should be individualized and context-driven. See the
  primary outcomes from **FOURIER** and its clinical summary for details.
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1615664">
    NEJM 2017 FOURIER
  </L>

</C>

<C>
  All of this points to one truth: you cannot judge health or disease risk
  through a single number. Even within cholesterol itself, no isolated value
  tells the full story. Labels like “good” or “bad” cholesterol oversimplify a
  system that is far more complex. Metabolic context dictates everything.
  Cholesterol, LDL, apoB, etc, are only pieces of a larger network of energy
  transport, inflammation, and repair. A low LDL does not guarantee protection,
  and a high one does not automatically put you on parole. What matters is how
  these systems interact, because the human body is not a mechanical device but
  a self-regulating, adaptive organism.
</C>

<C>
  We have covered a lot, from LDL and brain health to genetics and the LMHR
  discussion, and all of it leads to one question. How do you actually measure
  what is happening in your arteries? Did I mention the **Coronary Artery
  Calcium (CAC)** score earlier? It's the most reliable way to see what's truly
  going on inside your vessels.
</C>

<C>
  The CAC scan measures how much calcium has accumulated within your coronary
  arteries, giving a direct physical record of atherosclerosis already underway.
  Cholesterol levels can fluctuate daily with diet, stress, or medication, but
  calcium buildup develops slowly over time as the body tries to reinforce
  damaged arterial walls. A CAC score does not predict risk, it shows what has
  already happened. It is the closest thing we have to an X-ray of your
  cardiovascular history.
</C>

<C>
  A CAC score of zero carries strong reassurance. It usually means there is no
  calcified plaque and the short-term risk of a heart attack is very low, even
  lower than some people with “ideal” cholesterol numbers who still have early
  plaque. This is what researchers call the “Power of Zero.” As the score rises,
  the meaning shifts. Every increase reflects accumulated arterial injury and a
  higher chance of future cardiovascular events. Once you are above zero, the
  question becomes how much disease is already present and how fast it is
  progressing (
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9982666/">
    Power of Zero review 2022
  </L>
  ).
</C>{" "}

<C>
  If your **CAC score** is above zero or you have strong risk indicators such as
  family history, high **apoB**, or insulin resistance, then a{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/cir.0000000000000625">
    CT coronary angiography
  </L>{" "}
  (CCTA) can take you deeper. Unlike a CAC scan, which only detects hardened,
  calcified plaque, a CCTA provides a full, three-dimensional view of your
  coronary arteries. It uses contrast dye and advanced imaging to visualize both
  **calcified and non-calcified plaque**, giving a far more complete picture of
  your cardiovascular health.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  This is critical because non-calcified, or “soft,” plaque often represents the
  **earliest stage of atherosclerosis**. These plaques are more metabolically
  active and prone to rupture, which is what triggers most heart attacks. A
  person can have a CAC score of zero yet still harbor soft plaque that a
  calcium scan cannot see. CCTA captures that nuance by showing not only whether
  plaque exists but also **where it sits, how much there is, and what type it
  is**, fibrous, lipid-rich, or mixed.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  The advantage of coronary CT angiography is that it transforms risk estimation
  into direct visualization. Instead of guessing based on lab numbers, you can
  literally see whether disease is present and whether it is stable or
  progressing.
</C>

<C>
  Glucose control is one of the strongest predictors of long-term heart and
  metabolic health. Fasting glucose gives a snapshot, it tells you how much
  sugar is in your blood at that moment. But it can fluctuate depending on
  stress, sleep, or your last meal. That’s why it should always be paired with{" "}
  <L href="https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/a1c-test/">
    Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)
  </L>
  , a test that reflects your average blood sugar over the past 2 to 3 months. HbA1c
  rises when your blood sugar stays high for long periods, making it a better measure
  of chronic control. Studies show that even slightly elevated HbA1c levels, below
  the diabetic range, are linked to higher rates of heart disease and stroke. A healthy
  HbA1c is typically below 5.7%. <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/JAHA.123.031095">
    JAHA 2024
  </L>, <L href="https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/36/11/3759/37934/HbA1c-and-the-Risks-for-All-Cause-and">
    Diabetes Care 2013
  </L>, <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10612188/">
    Meta-analysis 2023
  </L>.
</C>

<C>
  Chronically high blood sugar leads to glycation, which means sugar molecules
  attach to proteins and fats. This triggers oxidative stress and chronic
  inflammation that slowly damage blood vessels. The inner lining of arteries,
  called the endothelium, becomes stiffer and more prone to plaque buildup,
  basically atherosclerosis. Even without diabetes, this cycle of mild
  inflammation and endothelial damage can set the stage for heart attacks and
  strokes.
</C>

<C>
  Insulin resistance is the thread that connects almost every major metabolic
  problem. Insulin is a hormone released by the pancreas that allows your cells
  to absorb glucose from the blood and use it for energy. When your cells stop
  responding properly to insulin, the pancreas tries to compensate by releasing
  more of it. This leads to a state where both insulin and glucose remain
  elevated in the blood, a condition that promotes inflammation, fat storage,
  and eventually metabolic disease.
</C>

<C>
  Researchers often estimate insulin resistance using something called the{" "}
  <L href="https://diabetesjournals.org/care/article/27/6/1487/22836/Use-and-Abuse-of-HOMA-Modeling">
    HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance)
  </L>{" "}
  score. It’s a simple calculation based on fasting glucose and fasting insulin
  levels. The higher your HOMA-IR, the more insulin resistant you are. Over
  time, high scores predict a greater risk of developing type 2 diabetes,
  cardiovascular disease, and fatty liver.
</C>

<C>
  The good news is that insulin resistance is reversible. Improving diet
  quality, getting regular movement, building muscle, sleeping well, and
  managing stress can restore insulin sensitivity and lower both insulin and
  glucose levels. Catching it early is key, because once insulin resistance
  takes hold, it quietly drives the same processes that cause heart disease,
  obesity, and metabolic decline.{" "}
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4016563/">
    Population study 2013
  </L>
  , <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10652621/">Review 2023</L>
</C>

<C>
  **hs-CRP** means high-sensitivity C-reactive protein. CRP is a protein made by
  the liver when the innate immune system is activated. The high-sensitivity
  assay detects very low levels in the blood and captures **chronic, low-grade
  vascular inflammation**. Mechanism in brief: cytokines like IL-6 signal the
  liver to produce CRP, which then rises in proportion to inflammatory activity
  within the vessel wall and elsewhere. Higher baseline hs-CRP consistently
  tracks with more cardiovascular events in large pooled analyses, and it can
  help **refine risk** in people who fall in the gray zone after standard
  scoring, although it is **not disease specific** and can rise with infections,
  obesity, or autoimmune disease. Thresholds commonly used in practice are about
  1 mg/L for lower risk and 3 mg/L for higher risk when measured outside of
  acute illness. See individual-participant-data meta-analysis in{" "}
  <L href="https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(09)61717-7/fulltext">
    Lancet 2010
  </L>{" "}
  and modeling from the Emerging Risk Factors Collaboration in{" "}
  <L href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa1107477">NEJM 2012</L>.
</C>

<C>
  Blood pressure. Hypertension accelerates atherosclerosis and stroke risk.
  Modern guidance defines hypertension at 130/80 mm Hg and targets less than
  130/80 mm Hg for most adults under treatment, especially when 10-year ASCVD
  risk is 10 percent or higher.{" "}
  <L href="https://www.acc.org/Latest-in-Cardiology/ten-points-to-remember/2017/11/09/11/41/2017-Guideline-for-High-Blood-Pressure-in-Adults">
    ACC summary

    Guideline text
  </L>.
</C>

<C>
  Waist circumference. Where you store fat matters. Abdominal or visceral fat
  raises cardiometabolic risk at any given body weight, which is why waist

<C>
  **Aerobic fitness** refers to how efficiently your heart, lungs, and muscles
  use oxygen during physical activity. It is often called **cardiorespiratory
  fitness (CRF)**, and it is one of the strongest predictors of how long you
  will live and how well your cardiovascular system ages. The higher your
  aerobic fitness, the better your body can deliver oxygen to working muscles
  and clear waste products like carbon dioxide and lactic acid.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  The term **MET** stands for **Metabolic Equivalent of Task**. One MET equals
  the amount of energy your body uses at rest. If an activity is rated at 10
  METs, it means you are burning energy at ten times your resting rate. Studies
  have shown that for every 1-MET increase in aerobic fitness, the risk of
  all-cause mortality and cardiovascular death decreases significantly. In other
  words, even small improvements in aerobic capacity can translate into
  meaningful extensions of lifespan.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  The gold-standard way to measure aerobic fitness is through a **VO₂ max
  test**, which measures the maximum volume of oxygen your body can consume per
  minute per kilogram of body weight. This test is usually done in a lab using a
  treadmill or cycle ergometer with a mask that measures inhaled and exhaled
  gases, (like that russian guy from Rocky IV). A higher VO₂ max indicates a
  more efficient cardiovascular system and better endurance capacity.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  If you do not have access to a lab VO₂ test, you can approximate your aerobic
  fitness through timed performance assessments. For example, record how fast
  you can climb several flights of stairs, row 2,000 meters, or run one mile,
  then retest periodically under similar conditions. Improvements in speed,
  recovery time, or heart rate response reflect higher cardiorespiratory
  efficiency. These practical tests align with the same physiological principles
  as VO₂ max, as demonstrated in a
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/1108396">
    2009 JAMA meta-analysis
  </L>
  and reinforced by a<L href="https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/58/10/556">2024 BJSM umbrella review</L>
</C>

<C>
  **Lp-PLA2**, short for **Lipoprotein-associated phospholipase A2**, is part of
  the same biological network that links lipid metabolism, oxidation, and
  vascular inflammation. It is an enzyme carried mostly on LDL particles,
  especially the small and oxidized ones. Its main function is to break down
  oxidized phospholipids on the surface of LDL. This reaction produces
  inflammatory molecules such as lysophosphatidylcholine and oxidized fatty
  acids, which attract immune cells and increase local inflammation in the
  arterial wall.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Because of this, Lp-PLA2 acts as a bridge between lipid oxidation and immune
  activation, the same process that turns oxidized LDL into foam cells and
  plaque. In population studies, higher levels of Lp-PLA2 activity or mass are
  linked with greater cardiovascular risk. It behaves more like a marker of
  ongoing vascular inflammation than a direct cause of disease.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Attempts to block this enzyme have not improved clinical outcomes. The drug
  **darapladib**, an Lp-PLA2 inhibitor, was tested in two large randomized
  trials, **SOLID-TIMI 52** for acute coronary syndrome and **STABILITY** for
  chronic coronary disease. Neither trial showed meaningful reductions in heart
  attacks, strokes, or cardiovascular deaths. These results suggest that while
  Lp-PLA2 reflects inflammation, it is not a central driver of atherosclerosis.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Because of these findings, major guidelines such as the **ACC/AHA 2019
  prevention guideline** do not recommend routine Lp-PLA2 testing or targeting
  it in treatment. It remains interesting from a biological standpoint though
  and can sometimes provide additional context, but it does not guide management
  in practice.
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/367933">
    JAMA 2014 SOLID-TIMI 52
  </L>
  <L href="https://www.acc.org/latest-in-cardiology/clinical-trials/2014/09/01/16/30/stability">
    STABILITY trial summary

  </L>, <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7685565/">

<C>
  Blood clotting dynamics also play a major role in cardiovascular risk, and
  <L href="https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/367933">
    **fibrinogen**
  </L>
  is one of the primary proteins responsible for clot formation. Elevated
  fibrinogen levels increase blood viscosity and promote hypercoagulability,
  meaning the blood is more prone to forming clots. This state raises the risk
  of thrombosis, where clots form inside blood vessels and can lead to heart
  attacks or strokes. Chronic inflammation, oxidative stress, and metabolic
  dysfunction ALL contribute to higher fibrinogen levels, making it an important
  marker for assessing not only clotting risk but also underlying inflammatory
  processes that drive cardiovascular disease.
</C>

<C>
  Not all lipoproteins behave the same way. Returning to **LDL**, there is a
  specific form called{" "}
  <L href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/ATVBAHA.108.179697">
    oxidized LDL
  </L>
  . This is LDL cholesterol that has undergone **oxidation**, a chemical modification
  caused by exposure to free radicals and oxidative stress. When LDL becomes oxidized,
  it changes structure and behaves differently inside blood vessels. The immune system
  no longer recognizes it as normal, so specialized white blood cells called **macrophages**
  engulf it.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Once macrophages consume large amounts of oxidized LDL, they transform into
  what are known as **foam cells**. These foam cells accumulate within the
  arterial wall and form the foundation of **atherosclerotic plaque**, the fatty
  buildup that narrows arteries and increases the risk of heart attacks. This
  process is one of the most thoroughly documented mechanisms in cardiovascular
  biology, confirmed in both animal models and human tissue studies.
</C>{" "}

<C>
  Blood tests can measure circulating levels of oxidized LDL, and studies often
  show that higher levels correlate with greater plaque burden or more advanced
  coronary disease. However, the clinical usefulness of routinely measuring
  oxidized LDL is still uncertain. It helps explain the *why* behind
  atherosclerosis but does not necessarily guide treatment decisions better than
  standard lipid and inflammation markers such as apoB, LDL-C, or hs-CRP. In
  other words, it is a valuable biological insight, not a primary diagnostic or
  therapeutic target.
</C>

<C>
  Myeloperoxidase (MPO) is an enzyme released by activated white blood cells
  during inflammation. It converts hydrogen peroxide into reactive oxidants used
  to destroy pathogens, but when overproduced, those oxidants also attack the
  body’s own lipids and vessel walls. MPO can oxidize LDL particles, turning
  them into oxidized LDL that sticks to arteries and accelerates plaque
  formation. High MPO levels therefore reflect active oxidative and inflammatory
  injury within the arteries and predict cardiovascular events. Despite its
  value, it's still mostly used in specialized cardiovascular labs because the
  assay is not standardized for mass testing.
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11274265/">
    MPO review 2024
  </L>
</C>

<C>
  **ADMA (asymmetric dimethylarginine)** and **SDMA (symmetric
  dimethylarginine)** interfere with the nitric oxide pathway, which controls
  blood vessel relaxation and vascular tone. Nitric oxide is produced by an
  enzyme called nitric oxide synthase that uses arginine as its substrate. ADMA
  directly inhibits this enzyme, reducing nitric oxide production, while SDMA
  competes with arginine for transport into cells, indirectly lowering its
  availability. High levels of either compound reduce nitric oxide output,
  causing vessel stiffness, poor endothelial function, and higher cardiovascular
  risk. Elevated dimethylarginines are also seen in kidney disease and metabolic
  syndrome, where clearance and metabolism are impaired.
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5035346/">
    Risk review 2016
  </L>

<C>
  F2-isoprostanes are compounds formed when free radicals oxidize fatty acids in
  cell membranes (arachidonic acid). They are direct byproducts of lipid
  peroxidation, meaning they indicate the real chemical damage caused by
  oxidative stress. Because they reflect oxidative reactions occurring inside
  tissues rather than just inflammation in the blood, they are among the most
  accurate markers of oxidative stress known in humans. However, they require
  advanced lab techniques such as gas chromatography–mass spectrometry for
  detection, which keeps them mainly in research use rather than clinical
  practice.
</C>

<C>
  **BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide)** and **NT-proBNP** are hormones secreted
  by the heart’s ventricles when they are under pressure or stretched due to
  fluid overload or weak contraction. They act as the heart’s self-protective
  signal to the kidneys, telling them to excrete sodium and water to reduce
  volume. The higher their concentration, the more stressed the heart muscle is.
  These peptides are crucial in diagnosing and monitoring heart failure and can
  predict future cardiovascular events, even in people who do not yet have
  symptoms.
  <L href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7280763/">
    NT-proBNP meta-analysis 2020
  </L>
</C>

<C>
  I could go on and on, but the point is, that modern diagnostics show how much
  more there is to cardiovascular health than one cholesterol number. The heart
  is not a pipe that clogs from eating fat. It is a living system shaped by
  thousands of biochemical signals that rise and fall with how you eat, move,
  sleep, and live. The balance between metabolism, inflammation, oxidative
  stress, coagulation, and vascular repair determines whether your arteries stay
  resilient or begin to decay. Assessing these systems together gives a far
  clearer picture of long-term health than any isolated marker. Reducing heart
  disease to LDL alone is not science, it is superstition.
</C>

<C>
  The tragedy is that public health messaging took complexity and turned it into
  dogma. Fat was vilified, cholesterol became the enemy, and people were told to
  replace real food with processed sugar, grains, and seed oils. The result was
  predictable. Obesity became normal, metabolic disease spread across every age
  group, and heart disease stayed the leading cause of death despite decades of
  drugs, diets, and slogans about “low-fat living.” None of it worked, because
  it never addressed the system itself.
</C>

<C>
  The way forward is not fear or blind loyalty to outdated rules. It is
  understanding. Learn how your body behaves, test what matters, and act on real
  data, not ideology.
</C>
